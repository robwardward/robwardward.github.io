I also found this essay on JSTOR written by Kay Halasek called Feminism and Bakhtin: Dialogic Reading in the Academy and she says that Kristeva looks to Bakhtins work and comes away with an interpretation for her feminist agenda. I mean, Kristeva using Bakhtins theory for its radical potential in her own work is no bad thing. But, I mean, dont you think that its something worth considering? That the way you read Bakhtin is kind of influenced by someone who had potentially more of a radical purpose than he did?

I think you should definitely check out Halaseks essay - it felt very pertinent to what youre thinking about - she writes her experience of reading Bakhtin as a woman, and feeling sort of alienated by his work and how she initially wanted to use Bakhtins theories to write a dissertation on pedagogy, but realised that by ignoring gender differences, Bakhtin overlooked issues about language and difference central to [her] concerns as a woman in academia.

I guess what Im saying is that maybe Bakhtins theory of a dialogism is a great point of departure for your piece, but that something about it doesnt entirely work, or that it becomes kind of problematic when we try to apply it to feminism? Jackson,

I have listened to you, and I have really tried to engage with the thinking that youve been doing. Now its time for you to listen to - not just hear - what I have to say, okay?

Ive been reading a lot of feminist writing these past few weeks with all of this in mind, trying to figure out exactly what was so problematic about your initial thesis, and I think it might be that youre the problem here. You making statements like dialogue is dialogue - that is the problem, Jackson. This became especially clear to me when I re-read Judith Butlers essay, Gender Trouble, theres this brilliant bit where she writes:

The very notion of dialogue is culturally specific and historically bound, and while one speaker may feel secure that a conversation is happening, another may be sure it is not. The power relations that condition and limit dialogic possibilities need first to be interrogated. Otherwise, the model of dialogue risks relapsing into a liberal model that assumes that speaking agents occupy equal positions of power and speak with the same presuppositions about what constitutes agreement and unity and, indeed, that those are the goals to be sought.

I mean, Butler just articulated there exactly what I needed to say to you.

What shes saying is that before we can embark on this idealistic route of a feminism that is always in dialogue, we first need to ask ourselves what we mean by dialogue, because the notion of dialogue is going to mean different things depending on where a person stands historically and culturally. Lets take you and I for example. Historically, you are in a position of power in this conversation because youre a man. Men have dominated dialogue, and so for them, dialogue is always happening when its between a man and a woman. Or for white people, they are going to be sure that a conversation is happening, while a person of color in that same conversation may beg to differ. Or take a conversation in feminism between cis-gendered and trans people. The individuals in that conversation stand on different ground, historically, in regards to their positions of power. So before we embark on that journey of a dialogic feminism, we first need to interrogate - or open a dialogue about - the very notion of dialogue.

Do you see now?

Lets think back to the quote from Bakhtin you were using to ground your thesis, about how past meanings can never be stable in the dialogic context and how they will always change (be renewed). The question in my mind here is: does change always mean renewal? Because change can mean replacing, too, and can ultimately result in forgetting past meanings. We cant forget the historical implications and consequences of what the notion of dialogue has meant for different individuals and different cultures. We can have new relationships to those meanings; we can - and should - be able to read history in nuanced ways, but we shouldnt replace it - we cant replace it. What I mean is, we cant forget the historical implications and consequences of what dialogue has meant for different individuals and within different cultures. Perhaps, as a white heterosexual man, you dont have the same stake in remembering history as some do, but there are some things that feminism needs to preserve - or - remember. If we forget that - historically - women havent had occupied a position of power in political dialogues as men have, then we risk, as Butler says, assuming that the speaking agents occupy equal positions of power. That is, assuming that dialogue means the same thing to both agents. Your thesis of a dialogic feminism assumes this, and so, for me, its ruined itself before it has even begun.

Its a great idea in theory, but the way youre approaching it (using a philosopher who isnt concerned with gender) just doesnt seem to work. I mean this is the whole problem! White men are doing the writing and the rewriting of meanings, and minorities arent being listened to. Im thinking here about University syllabi and the English canon and the current state of Western politics, where male thinkers and politicians have the predominant positions of power in changing the way we think, and in making changes to our society as a whole. Your thesis makes the assumption that the current model of dialogue can be beneficial to feminism, but how can it be if dialogue as we know it is dominated by men? How can change and regeneration benefit feminism if the very groups that feminism exists for arent the predominant ones actively making those changes? As it stands today, and Im sure youll agree, people of color arent being listened to. LGBTQ people are not being listened to. Women are not being listened to.
